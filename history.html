A BRIEF HISTORY of the STATE

Early History

Indian burial mounds throughout the state of Tennessee provide evidence of the earliest settlers here. Paleo-Indians are believed to have hunted and camped in what is now Tennessee as early as 12,000 years ago. Along with projectile points common for this period, archaeologists in Williamson County have uncovered a 12,000-year-old mastodon skeleton with cut marks typical of prehistoric hunters. Indian villages are found along the banks of most of the major rivers in Tennessee. These Indian tribes became part of the Cherokee nation.

The earliest written histories of Tennessee were penned in the mid-16th century, about the Hernando de Soto Expeditions. Later, French explorers and traders were in evidence, establishing a presence at Fort Assumption during the Indian Wars. A century later, Tennessee became the western counties of North Carolina, but after the Revolutionary War, North Carolina no longer wanted to continue trying to govern these distant counties. On June 1, 1796, Congress approved the admission of Tennessee the sixteenth state of the Union. Last out - First In

During the Civil War, Tennessee became the last state to officially withdraw from the Union, by the slimmest of votes. As a border state, Tennessee saw numerous battles, mostly won by the Union troops, and Nashville was the first Confederate state capital to fall to the Union. After the war, Tennessee was also the first state readmitted to the Union on July 24, 1866. It also was the only Confederate state that was not assigned a military governor during Reconstruction.

Some other 20th century historical items of note:

World War I Hero Alvin C. York
Alvin C. York was a formerly conscientious objector, who, in October 1918, subdued an entire German machine gun regiment in the Argonne Forest, receiving the Medal of Honor. His story was told in the movie Sergeant York. Image: Portrait from https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alvin_C._York

Scopes Trial
In 1925, John T. Scopes, a high school biology teacher, stood trial in Dayton, Tennessee for violating a law that forbade the teaching of evolution in the public schools. The Courthouse was turned into a circus of national and even international media coverage, to witness the high-powered legal counsels, William Jennings Bryan for the prosecution, and Clarence Darrow for the defense, argue their case. The story was told in the movie Inherit the Wind. Image: © 2005 Smithsonian Institution http://siarchives.si.edu/research/scopes.html

Martin Luther King Jr Assassination
On April 3, 1968, Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. was assassinated by James Earl Ray at the Lorraine Motel, in Memphis.
